The Best Fishing Horror Game Out There

Dredge is the rare game that earns the word uncanny - a fishing trip that quietly, deliberately, becomes something far more sinister than you signed up for.

Dredge, by Black Salt Games, is a modern masterpiece. It has a unique art style, a dark and layered story, and a generous pinch of cosmic horror holding everything together. You play as the Angler - catching fish, avoiding the horrors of the night, and collecting ruins for a mysterious, unsettling someone who has taken a peculiar interest in you.

It is an amazing game with simple but fleshed-out mechanics, and an alive, interactive world populated by 24 unique characters across five very different regions. Every single one of them has something to say - and more often than not, something strange to ask of you.

A World Full of Stories

In Dredge, you will meet many characters. Some will need you to catch fish and let them rot to prove a point. Others will ask you to serve as bait to lure out and destroy a sea monster. It is never a boring or drawn-out moment - the game is generous with its ideas and stingy with padding, which is precisely why it works so well.

The world of Dredge - beautiful on the surface, deeply unsettling beneath.

Sanity, Darkness & the Deep

The main mechanic Dredge builds itself around is sanity. As you use your cursed abilities and spend time in darkness - out at sea after dusk - you will lose your grip on reality, and things will begin to happen. Things you cannot fully explain.

  • Your lights go out, leaving you in blind darkness
  • Rocks turn invisible, the sea a silent minefield
  • A plague infests your cargo overnight
  • Birds descend and steal fish straight from your hold
  • Something in the water finds your boat - and eats it

As you go insane, things come to get you. Dredge never screams this at you with jump scares or scripted chases - instead, it lets dread do the work. The atmosphere is oppressive in the best possible way, and the game is extraordinarily disciplined about letting your imagination fill the gaps it leaves open.

The Joy of Fishing

Setting horror aside, the fishing itself is genuinely, surprisingly fun. There are many types of fishing minigames, each with their own requirements and rhythms - different rods unlock access to different species, and no two catches feel entirely the same. Then there is the inventory system, which deserves special mention.

Every item and piece of equipment takes up a specific shape in your boat's cargo grid. It is a puzzle game hiding inside a fishing game. Fish have impractical, awkward shapes. Equipment must occupy specific slots to function. For most of the game, space is the most precious resource you have - more limiting than money, more limiting than time.

The controls are best with a controller, but a keyboard works fine. Moving your boat feels so fluid and natural that returning to other games afterwards feels uncomfortably bulky - Dredge's navigation is one of those invisible design decisions you only notice once it's gone.

The Story - Where Dredge Truly Excels

Arguably the thing Dredge does best is its storytelling. You begin the game looking for a new position as an Angler, crash landing at the shores of a lighthouse. The town takes you in, hands you an old boat, and asks that you work off the cost. A simple, grounded start.

Not long after you set out, you will catch an aberration - a mutated, wrong-looking fish that has no business existing. When you sell it, you will meet someone you do not yet know is a manifestation of your guilt. This person - The Collector - gives you dredging equipment and asks just one thing in return: find five artifacts scattered across all 5 regions, and bring them to him. Each artefact grants you a new power. You accept, because what else would you do?

The five regions each carry their own stories, told through characters and environments rather than cutscenes or text walls. And when you have visited them all, collected everything The Collector asked for - it is time to face the truth.

Ending One
The Bad Ending

You travel with The Collector towards the open ocean. Following his instructions, you throw the five artefacts and a scarlet book into the sea - and out of the water, rising slowly through the dark, comes your dead wife.

The end of the world has begun. An eldritch horror awakens. Your main hub - the town that gave you shelter, the people who trusted you - burns while the credits roll. You got what you came for. It cost everyone else everything.

The cost of unchecked grief - the bad ending is harrowing in its quiet devastation.
🕯
Ending Two
The Good Ending

If you pay attention to the NPCs and the stories the world quietly tells you, you will have heard of an old mayor who one day simply went mad. If you find this old mayor and listen - really listen - you may find within yourself the ability to let go.

Returning to The Collector's manor, you punch him in the face. He shatters, leaving behind nothing but broken glass from a mirror - because that is all he ever was. A reflection. Your own guilt, given form.

Go to the lighthouse keeper - the same one at the lighthouse you crashed into at the very start of the game, all those hours ago. Ask for her help. Follow her light out into the open sea. Throw the scarlet book into the ocean, its rituals unperformed, its power spent. And then let the abominations of the deep take you. You are sacrificing yourself to spare everyone else from the weight of what you nearly did.

It is one of the most quietly devastating endings I have experienced in years. The game never raises its voice.

Final Thoughts

Dredge is a shortish game - eight to twelve hours on a casual playthrough - but not a single one of those hours is wasted. There is no waiting, no filler, no moment where you are simply passing time. There is always something to do, always something to discover, and if you want to go further, a full 100% completion run offers plenty of reason to stay.

The art style is beautiful in its simplicity. The gameplay is basic but never grows repetitive. The cover art is designed to resemble an oil painting, which tells you everything about the care behind this project. I would recommend Dredge to people who do not even like horror - because Dredge is not about horror in the way most games are. There are no jump scares. No scripted chases. Just atmosphere, and the creeping, earned dread of a world that is watching you from below.
